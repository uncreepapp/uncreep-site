Change Order Email Template: 3 Copy-Paste Emails Clients Actually Say Yes To

By Lakhwinder, founder of Uncreep · July 2026 · 6 min read

The hard part of charging for extra work isn't the decision. It's the email. You know the request is out of scope, you know it's worth $400, and you still stare at a blank reply box because every draft sounds either apologetic or hostile.

We've sent (and generated) a lot of these emails building Uncreep, and the ones that get a fast, cheerful "approved!" all share the same skeleton. Below: the skeleton, then three templates you can paste today.

What every change order email needs

Four beats, in this order:

  1. Enthusiasm first. Open with a genuine yes to the idea. The client should never feel the request itself was a mistake.
  2. Point at the scope, not at them. "This is outside what we spec'd" blames a document, not a person. That's why the document exists.
  3. A concrete price and timeline impact. One number, one date. Vague costs feel like gouging; specific costs feel like professionalism.
  4. A one-word yes. End with a question they can answer with "go ahead." No forms, no friction.

Notice what's missing: apologies. You are not sorry that work costs money, and the moment you sound sorry, the price becomes negotiable. Warm and unapologetic is the entire tone.

Template 1: The small add-on

For the classic "can you also just…" request. A new page, an extra graphic, a small feature. Send this the same day the request lands, while the idea is still exciting to them.

TEMPLATE 1 · SMALL ADD-ON
Subject: [Feature] — quick change order

Hi [Name],

Love this idea — a [contact form / extra page / new banner] would be a
genuinely good addition.

It sits outside the scope we agreed on ([e.g. "the five pages listed in
our proposal"]), so here's a quick change order rather than a surprise
on the final invoice:

  • Work: [one-line description]
  • Cost: $[amount]
  • Timeline: adds [X days] — new delivery date would be [date]

If that works, just reply "go ahead" and I'll fold it into this week.
If you'd rather keep the budget where it is, no problem at all — we
can park it for a phase two.

[Your name]
Why it works: the "park it for phase two" line gives them a graceful out. Clients who feel trapped argue about price. Clients with an exit ramp usually just approve.

Template 2: The mid-project pivot

For the big one: the rebrand mid-build, the "actually we want it to also do X," the new stakeholder with new opinions. Don't price this from your gut in the reply. Buy yourself a day to estimate properly.

TEMPLATE 2 · MID-PROJECT PIVOT
Subject: New direction — let's re-scope so nothing gets lost

Hi [Name],

Thanks for the update — I can see why [the new direction] makes sense
for where the business is heading, and I'm on board.

This is a bigger shift than a tweak, though: it affects [deliverables
affected], which were built against the original scope. Rather than
absorb that quietly and rush it, I'd like to do this properly:

  1. I'll put together a short change order by [tomorrow / date] with
     exact cost and a revised timeline.
  2. We pause work on the affected pieces until you approve it, so
     you're never billed for anything you haven't signed off on.
  3. Everything already delivered under the original scope stays as
     agreed.

Rough early read: expect roughly $[range] and [X] extra days, but the
document tomorrow will be exact.

Sound good?

[Your name]
Why it works: pausing work is not a threat, it's protection framed as a favor ("so you're never billed for anything you haven't approved"). And giving a rough range up front means the real number tomorrow won't be a shock.

Template 3: The revision limit

For round four of a two-round revision agreement. This is the awkwardest one, because each individual tweak is tiny. The trick is to charge for rounds, not tweaks.

TEMPLATE 3 · EXTRA REVISIONS
Subject: Round [3] — one quick note before I dive in

Hi [Name],

Got your notes — all doable, and honestly the [specific change] will
make it better.

Quick heads-up before I start: our agreement includes [two] rounds of
revisions, and we used those on [date] and [date]. Extra rounds are
billed at $[amount] per round (a round = one consolidated list of
changes), so if there are other tweaks brewing, batch them into this
list and they're all covered under the one round.

Reply "go ahead" and I'll have this round back to you by [date].

[Your name]
Why it works: "batch your other tweaks into this round" flips the frame. You're not charging them more, you're teaching them how to get more value per round. Revision requests get organized overnight.

Three rules for sending these

  • Send fast. A change order within an hour of the request reads as "this is my normal process." The same email three days later reads as "I stewed about this."
  • Never do the work first. The quote comes before the work, every time. Post-hoc invoices are where disputes live.
  • Keep a paper trail. "Go ahead" in an email is your approval record. If the yes happens on a call, follow up with "Confirming from our call: [scope, price, date]."
